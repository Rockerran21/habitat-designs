Architecture & hospitality — Bharatpur, Chitwan

A practice that builds the way Nepal hosts.

Habitat Design Studio designs farmstays, riverside restaurants and courtyard houses across the inner Terai — buildings that welcome you in the way a good house does, through light, airflow and the patience to leave a place looking like itself.

Shaped by climate, connectivity and the quiet logic of the landscape. Built with minimal intervention, and comfort that comes from light, materials, proportion and airflow.

Selected work

Four places we have hosted into being.

Two built and photographed, one in design, one held as a concept — each marked honestly, and each shaped first by where it stands.

01 Built

Himalchuli Nature Farmstay

Chitwan · Hospitality

Tiered clay-tile roofs step down until the building all but vanishes into dense sal forest. The trees were here first; they stay. Guests arrive into shade and birdsong before they ever see a wall — hospitality that begins with the ground.

NP Residence

Chitwan · Private residence

Rooms gather around a courtyard screened by a perforated timber jaali, with arched plaster niches and an internal garden carrying light and air deep into the plan. A veranda, a skylight and a common lobby keep the household cool through the long Terai afternoons.

03 Built

Bagar Restaurant

Riverside Chitwan · Restaurant

Named for the ground it stands on — bagar, the gravel bar. The riverbank's own topography stays in the plan; raw log columns carry hand-sculpted organic capitals, and river stones from the site sit in the plinth. You eat where the water has always been.

The studio

We host a building into the land.

Raw and minimal — we integrate what already exists rather than erasing it.

Habitat Design Studio works from Bharatpur, in the subtropical floodplain of the inner Terai — flat land, big rivers, three wet months a year. The climate writes the first lines of every brief.

Our instinct is to keep a site looking like itself. At Bagar the gravel bar stayed in the plan; at Himalchuli the sal forest closed over the roofs. Comfort comes not from machinery but from proportion, deep eaves, cross-ventilation and masonry that holds the night's cool into the afternoon — the same things that make a house feel like a welcome rather than a building.

  • Climate

    Openings sized for the low winter sun and shaded from the high summer one; cross-ventilation drawn before any compressor.

  • Materials

    Fired brick, sal and sisau timber, lime plaster, stone from the river bed — left to read as themselves, never disguised.

  • Water

    Plinth heights set by the rivers' record years, not their averages. The Narayani and the Rapti are designed for, not against.

  • Welcome

    A courtyard, a veranda, a shaded threshold — the architecture of arriving, so a place greets you before you reach the door.
