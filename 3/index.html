The Lead Photograph — From the Current Issue

Bagar — a restaurant raised on the gravel bar it is named for.

This issue’s lead photograph: Bagar Restaurant, on the riverside in Chitwan — whole logs for columns, hand-sculpted capitals above them, river stones in the plinth, and the bank left looking like itself.

In Nepali, a बगर — bagar — is the gravel bar a river leaves behind: flat, stony, open ground that most builders level and bury before the first wall goes up. The studio did the opposite. The restaurant takes the bank as the river made it — the existing topography runs straight through the plan, the plinth is ringed with stones gathered off the bar, and the columns are whole logs, each capital sculpted by hand into an organic form that no other column repeats.

The working creed is raw and minimal: integration of what a site already has, rather than its erasure. Buildings here are shaped by climate, connectivity, and the quiet logic of the landscape — built with minimal intervention, their comfort drawn from light, materials, proportion, and airflow. Bagar is the creed at its plainest. When the work was done, the riverbank still looked like itself.

Bagar Restaurant appears as No. 001 in the index, below.

No. 02

The Studio

Notes from Bharatpur: who we are, what we build with, and how close we stay to the work.

Practice
Architecture, interiors, and construction supervision — usually together.
Field
Chitwan and the inner Terai — Bharatpur, the riverside, Madi.
Materials
Whole-log timber, river stone, clay tile, perforated timber screens, lime plaster.
Site visits
Weekly, in every season — the monsoon especially.

Habitat Design Studio is an architecture and design practice working from Bharatpur, Chitwan. The work runs from a restaurant on a riverbank to a farmstay lost in the sal, and houses gathered around courtyards — all of it shaped by climate, connectivity, and the quiet logic of the landscape. The creed is raw and minimal: integrate what a site already has rather than erase it, intervene as little as the brief allows, and let comfort come from light, materials, proportion, and airflow.

The studio’s habit is closeness. We measure the site ourselves at the hottest hour of the day, sit with clients at their own table, and stay with the contractor through every pour, every course of stone, every capital sculpted onto its log. Most commissions arrive through Instagram and by word of mouth — from people who have eaten at Bagar or Kinar in June and noticed the difference.

A Note on Craft

Two traditions stand behind the work in this issue. From the Tharu villages of the Terai: Mokha, bas-relief in clay — peacocks, vines, lotus — sculpted by hand, almost always by women, and renewed each Tihar. From the Newar builders of the valley: tiki jhya, lattice windows assembled from interlocking carved battens, with no nails anywhere in them.

Neither is a museum piece here. The courtyard of NP Residence (No. 004 in the index) is walled with a full screen of perforated timber — the jaali doing what the tiki jhya has always done: air and light through, glare and eyes kept out. Where a project allows, we commission the work from working artisans, and the budget line says so plainly.

No. 03

Method

Every commission follows the same printed procedure. Clients receive this page with the first fee note.

  1. I.

    Survey & Brief

    We walk the plot at the hottest hour, and record levels, trees, bore results, and the neighbours’ shadows. The brief is written down and signed by both sides.

  2. II.

    Design

    Sketch plans through three sittings. Decisions are made over models and material samples — brick in the hand, not only on the screen.

  3. III.

    Drawings & Permits

    Working drawings with structural coordination, then approval through Bharatpur Metropolitan City. We carry the file to the ward office ourselves.

  4. IV.

    Tender & Site

    Priced with two or three contractors we have built with before. A studio architect is on site weekly; masonry, lattice, and joinery are checked against the drawings, course by course.

  5. V.

    Handover

    The snag list is closed before the keys move. As-built drawings are bound and delivered with a maintenance calendar set to the monsoon, not the calendar year.

“The procedure is deliberately dull; the buildings are where the interest goes.” — Margin note, studio copy
